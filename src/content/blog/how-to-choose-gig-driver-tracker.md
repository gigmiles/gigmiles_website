Most "gig driver apps" are really just mileage loggers with a nice dashboard. They count your miles and add up your gross — which is genuinely useful at tax time, but it's also the number the delivery app already showed you. If your goal is to know what you *actually keep* and make better decisions because of it, the tool has to do more than log trips. Here's the checklist that separates a real net-profit tracker from a glorified odometer — use it whichever app you end up with.

## 1. Does it show your NET, or just your gross?

This is the whole game. A lot of trackers aggregate your earnings across platforms and stop there. That's a bigger version of the same misleading number. What you want is take-home: gross minus your real vehicle cost minus an estimated tax set-aside. A $235 gross day is closer to $175 in your pocket — a tracker that never surfaces that $60 gap isn't tracking profit, it's tracking revenue.

**Look for:** a clear "real net" figure per shift and per week, not just a gross total.

## 2. Does it cost your ACTUAL car, or a generic number?

Two drivers doing identical miles can have wildly different real costs — a fuel-efficient sedan versus a thirsty SUV aren't in the same universe. Many apps apply one flat per-mile figure to everyone (or just the IRS rate, which is a *tax deduction*, not your real cash cost). Your real cost per mile depends on your specific vehicle's fuel economy, its depreciation curve, and category-based wear.

**Look for:** cost math tied to your actual make/model, not a one-size-fits-all rate. (Leasing? Then depreciation isn't your cost — a good tool knows that too.)

## 3. Per-hour AND per-mile — not just hourly

Hourly earnings hide expensive, high-mileage shifts; dollars-per-mile hides long unpaid waits between orders. Experienced drivers watch both, because a "good hourly" built on 200 deadhead miles isn't good at all.

**Look for:** real net profit shown **per hour and per mile**, so you can see which shifts and which orders actually pay.

## 4. Is the tax set-aside state-aware, or a flat guess?

"Set aside 25% for taxes" is a rule of thumb, not a number. Nine states have no income tax at all; others run progressive brackets that change what each extra gig dollar costs you. A flat percentage over- or under-shoots depending on where you live.

**Look for:** an estimated set-aside that factors in self-employment tax *and* your state — not a single blanket percentage. (It's an estimate for planning, always — your actual situation and a licensed tax professional have the final word.)

## 5. Does it understand a day job? (W-2 stacking)

This one almost every tool ignores. If you drive on weekends but have a W-2 job during the week, your gig income doesn't get taxed in a vacuum — it stacks on top of your wages and is taxed at your *marginal* rate. A tracker that assumes your gig income is your only income can badly misjudge what to set aside.

**Look for:** the ability to account for other (W-2) income when it estimates your tax slice. It's rare, and it matters if gig work is your side hustle.

## 6. If you're on an e-bike, does it do the math *right*?

Here's a trap: you **cannot** use the IRS standard mileage rate on an e-bike — that rate is for cars, vans, and trucks. E-bike couriers deduct actual expenses instead: electricity, battery wear, and mechanical wear, which usually work out to around 6 cents a mile. A tracker that just applies car-mileage logic to your bike is giving you a number that isn't yours — and most of them do exactly that.

**Look for:** genuine e-bike support built on the actual-expense method, not a car formula wearing a bike costume. This is the single most under-served corner of the whole category.

## 7. Will your tax pro actually accept the output?

Come spring, the goal is a handoff, not a shoebox. Clean records — a mileage log, categorized expenses, receipt images, and a Schedule C–style summary you can export — turn tax season into an administrative task. A tool that keeps everything locked inside a dashboard you can't export isn't saving you the hard part.

**Look for:** receipt capture, categorized expenses, and an export (CSV/PDF or a Schedule C worksheet) your tax professional can use.

## 8. Does it compare platforms honestly?

If you multi-app, you want to know which platform actually pays best *after* costs — and you want that comparison to be honest, not skewed by one lucky $30 tip. Small sample sizes lie.

**Look for:** a per-platform net comparison with some sample-size sanity built in, so one good night doesn't crown the wrong app.

## The short version

A mileage logger tells you how far you drove. A real net-profit tracker tells you what you kept, on your car, in your state, per hour and per mile — and handles the e-bike case correctly if that's you. Run any app you're considering against the eight questions above; the gap between "logs miles" and "shows profit" is where all the useful decisions live.
