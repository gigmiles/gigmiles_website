The app says you're making $29 an hour. Your bank account, at the end of the month, tells a quieter story. If you drive for rideshare, food delivery, grocery, or package delivery, the hourly number on your screen is a **gross** hourly wage — before the car takes its share and before the taxes nobody withheld. Your **real** hourly wage is what's left. Here's how to find it, and why it changes which orders you should take.

## Two hourly wages, not one

Every gig driver actually earns two different hourly rates at the same time:

- **Gross hourly** — total payout ÷ hours worked. This is the number the app is happy to show you.
- **Real (net) hourly** — take-home ÷ hours worked, after vehicle costs and an estimated tax set-aside.

The gap between them is the whole game. Take a single, realistic shift:

| Line | Amount |
|---|---|
| Gross earnings (8 hours) | $235 |
| Vehicle costs (fuel $17 + wear & tear $26) | −$43 |
| Estimated tax set-aside (planning estimate) | −$17 |
| **Take-home** | **$175** |

On paper that's **$29 an hour**. In your pocket it's about **$22 an hour** — a **74% keep rate**. Same shift, two numbers roughly seven dollars an hour apart. Multiply that gap across a year of driving and it's the difference between "this is worth it" and "why am I doing this."

*(Every figure here is an illustrative example for planning, not tax advice or an earnings claim. Your real numbers depend on your car, your city, and your full tax situation.)*

## The reason the hourly number lies: it's really a per-mile number

Hours are how you *feel* the job. Miles are how it actually *costs* you. In that same example, 105 miles carried the whole shift, and the car's share of the cost worked out to roughly **41 cents a mile** (that's the $43 in fuel and wear, spread over 105 miles). The IRS, for what it's worth, assumes an even higher figure — the 2026 standard mileage rate is **76 cents per mile** (as of July 1, 2026) to cover fuel, maintenance, tires, depreciation, and insurance in one number.

So the miles hit you twice:

1. **Now**, as real money in gas and wear.
2. **Later**, as the mileage record that supports your deduction at tax time — *if* you logged them the day you drove, not reconstructed them in April.

Once you see the cost as per-mile, the hourly wage stops being mysterious. A slow shift where you drove 120 miles for $180 isn't "a bad hour" — it's too many paid-nothing miles.

## Turning your real number into a decision

This is where knowing your real hourly actually pays for itself: **order selection.**

Drivers repeat a rule of thumb — aim for offers worth at least about a dollar per mile, pickup and dropoff included. It's a decent starting filter, but it's a folk number, not *your* number. Your real floor depends on what your specific car costs to run. A fuel-efficient sedan and a thirsty SUV have very different break-even points, and an order that's profitable in one loses money in the other.

The move is to know the two things that set your floor:

- **Your cost per mile** — roughly what fuel plus wear costs to move your specific car one mile.
- **Your tax set-aside** — the slice of every dollar you keep aside so quarterly taxes aren't a shock. A common planning guideline is around **14 cents on every dollar earned**, but your actual rate depends on your income, filing status, and state.

An offer that clears both — covers the miles *and* leaves something after the set-aside — is a real earner. One that doesn't is the app paying you to depreciate your own car.

## The three things that quietly wreck the real hourly

- **Deadhead miles.** The drive to the pickup and the drive home between orders are unpaid but fully costed. They don't show up in the offer; they show up in your gas tank.
- **Waiting time.** Minutes parked outside a restaurant are hours on your clock but zero on your odometer *and* zero on your payout — they drag the hourly down without adding miles.
- **The forgotten expenses.** Gas is the one everyone counts. Depreciation, the work share of your phone plan, parking, tolls, and the work portion of insurance are the ones that vanish by tax season and make the "real hourly" look better than it is.

## How to actually know your real hourly

You don't need a spreadsheet habit. You need five things recorded consistently:

- Miles per shift, logged the day you drive them
- Gross payout per platform, per shift
- Gas and vehicle expenses, with dates
- Phone, parking, tolls, and other work expenses
- A running estimated tax set-aside, so April is a handoff and not an emergency

Do that and two things happen. During the year, you can see your real hourly climb or fall and adjust which shifts and orders you take. At tax time, you hand clean numbers to a licensed tax professional instead of a shoebox of guesses.

## The takeaway

There is nothing wrong with the money in gig work — but the number on the screen is the *before* picture, and the decisions that matter are made on the *after* picture. Know your real hourly and your real cost per mile, and you stop being surprised by your own bank account.
